Lauter & Sparge Removing the spent grain

Lauter is the term for straining the spent grain from the now sweetened liquid, called 'wort.' Sparging is the process of gently rinsing the grain with very hot water to extract as much fermentable sugar as possible. These two things are carried out simultaneously as one procedure. It is a delicate operation that gets easier with practice, but with care, even your first lauter and sparge can go smoothly.

  • Mash Out

    Once the mash is complete the temperature is raised to 170 - 175 degrees F to stop enzyme activity. Many brewers add hot water or raise the heat in order to accmplish this, but in my experince it is just as effective to begin the lauter and sparge process with water just under 200 degrees F which will cool rapidly from air contact during all the slow liquid transfer that takes place during this stage.

  • Settling the Mash Bed and Recirculating

    The number one concern during the lauter is a stuck mash. Avoiding a stuck mash is not really complicated, but it does require paying attention to the lauter and sparge. The idea is to keep the solid grain particles loosely floating in the liquid and not packed down into the bottom. With that in mind the first step, is to get mostly water and less grain on the bottom. If you're lautering in your mash tun, slowly run the sparge water into the top of the mash and throughly stir the whole mash. Before the mash settles and while the sparge water continues to run, slowly open the drain at the bottom of the mash tun so that the rate of flow in is approximately equal to the rate of flow out. If you are transferring the mash into a separate lauter tun, you will do the same thing but first you will need to pour a layer of water into the bottom until it is 3 - 4 inches above the drainage system and then alternate gently scooping in layers of grain with layers of more water.

    In addition to removing the grain, lautering also filters and clarifies the wort. To make this effective the first gallon or two, which will be quite hazy, is not transferred to the boil kettle but poured back into the top of the mash. It's important to pour gently in order to not disturb the grain bed. You can stop recirculating when the liquid runs clear.

  • Lautering

    Continue running the sparge water through the grain bed taking care to maintain the in flow rate equal to the out flow rate. It will save a lot of time if you can start the boil before the lauter and sparge are complete.

    Do Not allow the level of the water to fall below the top of the grain bed before the end of the lauter.

  • When to Stop

    Periodically take samples of the wort through out the lauter and test its specific gravity which will steadily fall through out the process. When the specific gravity drops below 1.008 stop. If you run out of hot water before you reach this point, you are effectively done. Usually set aside 0.5 gallons of water per pound of grist. It's not a bad idea to add an extra gallon.
